V0.1 — Doctrine of Discovery

In 1452, a Vatican decree outlined permanent servitude for non-Christian peoples. In 1493, another decree divided non-Christian lands between European crowns. In 1823, the U.S. Supreme Court referenced this Doctrine of Discovery when shaping federal land law. In 1857, the same court ruled that Black Americans held no legal rights. In 1896, it upheld racial separation. In 1988, the U.S. government issued a formal apology and reparations for wartime internment. In 2023, the Vatican withdrew the 1493 decree. Each step was recorded. The archive is public.

This shows how legal ideas about land and people changed over time — and how those ideas are still part of today’s rules.

V0.2 — Cultural Reduction

In adult media, racialized performers are disproportionately cast in fetishized roles. The 'fantasy' often encodes dominance, submission, and racial hierarchy. In education, Black inventors filed nearly 50,000 patents between 1870 and 1940. Their contributions remain largely absent from textbooks. From 1920 to the present, non-white innovation has been systematically minimized in scholastic literature. The pattern is structural. The record is public.

This shows how stories and inventions from people of color were often left out — and how that shaped what we learn and watch.
